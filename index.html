Deal Scoring

The Deal Score — how it works

Every auction is assigned a score from 0 to 100. Three factors determine the score — weighted by their impact on investment returns.

Discount Depth
Primary Signal
How far the starting price sits below the court-appraised value. The larger the gap, the higher the score. The strongest single predictor of investment upside.
Auction Round
Round Signal
Second-round auctions begin at a lower legal minimum — a structural advantage most investors overlook. intelEstate flags these automatically.
Urgency
Time Signal
Time remaining until the auction date. Surfaces imminent opportunities so you can act before other investors have even opened the listing.

FAQ

Everything you need to know

intelEstate pulls data directly from the official Polish bailiff portal (licytacje.komornik.pl) and regional court announcement systems — not from third-party aggregators or secondary sources. Every listing is tied to a real court case reference number (KM/KMP), which you can independently verify. This means the data is legally authoritative and traceable to its source.
Our scraper runs every night and new listings typically appear in the system within 24 hours of being published on the official court portals. Polish law requires auctions to be publicly announced at least two weeks before the auction date, which gives you meaningful preparation time. PRO subscribers additionally receive early alerts for any HOT deal with an auction date within 3 days — so nothing slips through at the last minute.
Every auction is scored 0–100 using three weighted factors: discount vs the court-appraised value (60%), auction round — second-round auctions start at a lower price and score higher (20%), and urgency — how many days remain until the auction date (20%). Scores of 85 or above are flagged as HOT, 70–84 as GOOD. Road shares and non-investable property types are automatically excluded from scoring so they never pollute your feed.
intelEstate covers all 16 Polish voivodeships, with the densest auction activity in Małopolska, Mazowieckie, Dolnośląskie, and Śląskie. Coverage is national — we do not focus only on major cities, which means you can find exceptional deals in secondary markets that most investors overlook. EU expansion to Czech Republic and Hungary is on the roadmap for 2026.
Most Polish court auctions take place in person at the district courthouse in the property's jurisdiction — remote or online bidding is not yet standard procedure. However, you can appoint a legal representative (pełnomocnik) with a notarised power of attorney to bid on your behalf. EU and non-EU investors can both participate; non-EU buyers may need a Ministry of Interior permit for certain property types such as agricultural land.
If a first-round court auction receives no qualified bids, it is rescheduled as a second-round auction with a lower starting price — reduced from 75% to 66.7% of the court-appraised value. This means the floor price is automatically 11% lower than the first round before any competition even begins. Second-round auctions also tend to attract fewer bidders, which further increases your chances of securing the property at or near the starting price.
